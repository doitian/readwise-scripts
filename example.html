“If you can’t think of three things that might go wrong with your plans, then there’s something wrong with your thinking” (Rothman, 2022, p. 40)

“↩︎

- Two alternatives is a false choice—a dilemma.
- Three alternatives offer a real choice. In addition, it’s a way to create more alternatives.

“3.4 Hook The Reader with a Problem” (Rothman, 2022, p. 42) .h2

“People read nonfiction to see how they might solve problems. Start a piece by sharing the problem as a way to set the context, so the reader can see if this piece is for him or her.” (Rothman, 2022, p. 42)

“Here are three ways to hook the reader with a problem: • One Startling Sentence • Hey! You! See! So! • A story that shows someone similar to the ideal reader in a setting.” (Rothman, 2022, p. 42)

“3.4.1 One Startling Sentence” (Rothman, 2022, p. 42) .h3

“One Startling Sentence consists of a four-sentence paragraph in this order: • First sentence: What’s the problem? • Second sentence: Why is this problem a problem? (Note: this is what matters to the reader.) • Third sentence: The startling sentence. • Fourth sentence: Implication of the startling sentence.” (Rothman, 2022, p. 42)

“3.4.2 Hey! You! See! So!” (Rothman, 2022, p. 43) .h3

“Hey! get the reader to pay attention. • You! is why the reader should care about this issue—what matters. • See? is the one or two facts that you have—that no one else has. • So... is why the reader should care.” (Rothman, 2022, p. 43)

“3.5 Structure Your Piece with Logic” (Rothman, 2022, p. 45) .h2

“3.5.1 Budrys’ 7-Point Plot Outline” (Rothman, 2022, p. 46) .h3

“Points 1, 2, 3: A character, in a situation, with a problem. These points set the context for the rest of the piece.” (Rothman, 2022, p. 46)

“Points 4 and 5 are called “try/fail” cycles. In fiction, the protagonist tries something and fails.” (Rothman, 2022, p. 46)

“Point 6 is the climax of the piece. When someone uses your solutions, this is how they succeed.” (Rothman, 2022, p. 46)

“Point 7 is the validation. Since I suggest you finish the piece with a reminder of the opening, the reader feels satisfied.” (Rothman, 2022, p. 46)

“3.5.2 Three Possibilities” (Rothman, 2022, p. 47) .h3

“1. Identify the current state, with any of the openings back in “Hook the Reader with a Problem.” 2. Explain three possibilities in the form of tips, ideas, or approaches that will help the reader solve the problem(s) in the current state. 3. Finish the piece with a new state that helps the reader reflect back to the opening.” (Rothman, 2022, p. 47)

“What if you only have two possibilities? Add a third section explaining when your ideas won’t work.” (Rothman, 2022, p. 48)

“What if you have nine possibilities? Some possibilities: • Write one article with three groups of three possibilities. • Write three articles, each with one group of three. • Write nine articles, treating each possibility separately.” (Rothman, 2022, p. 48)

“3.5.3 The Pyramid Principle” (Rothman, 2022, p. 48) .h3

“The Pyramid starts with this introduction: • Situation: Define the current state. • Complication: What’s the change? • Question: What’s the hypothesis or questions we need to answer? • Answer: State the solution.” (Rothman, 2022, p. 49)

“4. Edit Just Enough” (Rothman, 2022, p. 54) .h1

“When you edit, you make your piece more accessible to your ideal reader. That’s it. Not perfection, because there is no such thing as perfect writing.” (Rothman, 2022, p. 54)

“Edit as soon as you think you’re done writing. If your editing causes you to think and learn, return to writing forward and cycling. Use that new thinking to write and cycle, continuing to integrate your thinking and learning as you proceed.” (Rothman, 2022, p. 55)
